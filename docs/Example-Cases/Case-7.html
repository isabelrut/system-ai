the Digital Product Passport

Version 4.0

Each requirement consists of an ID, a statement (the actual requirement itself), a rationale (a compliance-oriented reason behind the requirement), organization benefits (such as improved efficiency), a source (which are either published EU documents or EU working group documents), a priority (based on the MoSCoW method, which uses must/should/could/won't), a risk of implementation (about the possible complications that might occur), and system validation success criteria (to verify whether you adhere to this requirement).
Note that the DPP regulations are still bound to change. Hence, it is the users reponsibility to keep track of these changes.
This set of requirements has been generated using a Large Language Model. Although the used LLM has access to a relevant set of documents about the Digital Product Passport, it is still sensitive to hallucinations. Hence, it is recommended to only use these requirements as a starting point in your journey with the DPP. No rights can be derived from this LLM output.

The figure shows a digital maturity model that starts at incomplete, meaning there has been no digitalization in the company. Next, performed means that a planning has been made. Managed focuses on ensuring existing business processes are digitized through technology. Established means that digital transformation has been done based on existing standard. Predictable seizes those benefits by applying quantitative techniques on real-time data, with data analytics on business processes. Lastly, optimizing focuses on innovation and continuous improvement.

ID 1 Statement: The organization must collect and maintain product information necessary for the Digital Product Passport (DPP) for each textile product it supplies. Risk: Low – data collection is straightforward at this stage, but failure to collect may lead to non-compliance in the future.

Rationale: To ensure transparency and traceability of textile products in line with the EU strategy for sustainable and circular textiles.
Organization benefits: Enables compliance with EU regulations and supports sustainable practices.
Source: M2, M3
Priority: Must – compliance with future ESPR obligations is mandatory.

System Verification Success Criteria: All product information is documented and stored in a format that can be shared with downstream economic operators.

ID 2 Statement: The organization must assign a unique identifier to each textile product to enable tracking and access to its DPP. Risk: Medium – initial setup may require process changes, but the risk of non-compliance is high.

Rationale: Unique identifiers are essential for traceability and accessibility of the DPP as required by the Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation.
Organization benefits: Facilitates product tracking and supports supply chain collaboration.
Source: M3, M4
Priority: Must – without a unique identifier, the DPP cannot be linked to the product.

System Verification Success Criteria: Each product has a unique identifier that is consistently applied and recorded.

ID 3 Statement: The organization must ensure that the data collected for the DPP is accurate, up-to-date, and relevant to the product’s sustainability and circularity. Risk: Medium – data accuracy depends on process discipline, which may be challenging for small organizations.

Rationale: Accurate and relevant data supports informed decision-making and meets the EU’s sustainability goals.
Organization benefits: Enhances credibility and supports compliance with future regulations.
Source: M1, M2
Priority: Must – inaccurate data may lead to incorrect product assessments and legal consequences.

System Verification Success Criteria: Data validation checks are in place and data is verified by at least one independent party.

ID 4 Statement: The organization must ensure that the DPP data is accessible to downstream economic operators in a structured, machine-readable format. Risk: Medium – requires a basic digital infrastructure, which may be challenging for an organization with incomplete digital maturity.

Rationale: Accessibility is a core requirement of the ESPR to ensure transparency across the supply chain.
Organization benefits: Facilitates collaboration and reduces the administrative burden on downstream partners.
Source: M3, M4
Priority: Must – failure to provide accessible data may result in non-compliance.

System Verification Success Criteria: DPP data is shared in a standard format (e.g., JSON or XML) upon request.

ID 5 Statement: The organization must establish a process for updating the DPP data when changes occur in the product’s lifecycle, such as material composition or production method. Risk: Low – process can be manual and low-tech, but must be consistently followed.

Rationale: Lifecycle changes must be reflected in the DPP to maintain its relevance and accuracy.
Organization benefits: Ensures ongoing compliance and supports product innovation.
Source: M3, M4
Priority: Must – outdated DPP data may mislead stakeholders and lead to regulatory non-compliance.

System Verification Success Criteria: A documented procedure is in place for updating DPP data, and updates are logged.

ID 6 Statement: The organization must ensure that the DPP data is shared with downstream economic operators without unnecessary delay. Risk: Low – can be managed through simple communication protocols.

Rationale: Timely sharing supports the transparency and traceability goals of the ESPR and supports the circular economy.
Organization benefits: Builds trust with partners and supports supply chain efficiency.
Source: M3, M4
Priority: Must – delays in sharing may disrupt supply chain operations and lead to non-compliance.

System Verification Success Criteria: DPP data is shared within 7 business days of request.

ID 7 Statement: The organization must ensure that the DPP data includes at a minimum the product’s material composition, origin of raw materials, and production location. Risk: Low – data can be collected manually and stored in a basic format.

Rationale: These data elements are essential for assessing the environmental and social impact of the product.
Organization benefits: Supports informed decision-making and compliance with future regulations.
Source: M1, M3
Priority: Must – these are core data elements required under the ESPR.

System Verification Success Criteria: All products have DPP data containing the required elements.

ID 8 Statement: The organization must ensure that the DPP data is stored securely and is protected against unauthorized access or modification. Risk: Medium – requires basic data security measures, which may be unfamiliar to small organizations.

Rationale: Data security is essential to maintain the integrity and trustworthiness of the DPP.
Organization benefits: Reduces the risk of data breaches and supports legal compliance.
Source: M4, M5
Priority: Must – unauthorized access may lead to data corruption or misuse.

System Verification Success Criteria: Data is stored in a password-protected system or document, with access limited to authorized personnel.


ID 9 Statement: The organization should provide the DPP data in a format that is compatible with the digital systems used by downstream economic operators, such as retailers or brands. Risk: Low – can be achieved by using standard file formats like CSV or PDF.

Rationale: Compatibility ensures seamless integration and ease of processing, reducing the administrative burden on partners and improving supply chain efficiency.
Organization benefits: Strengthens relationships with downstream partners and supports smoother supply chain operations.
Source: O4, O5
Priority: Should – compatibility supports ecosystem-level compliance and collaboration, which is a key goal for the user.

System Verification Success Criteria: At least one downstream partner confirms receipt and successful processing of the DPP data.

ID 10 Statement: The organization should include additional sustainability markers, such as carbon footprint or fair trade certification, if available, in the DPP. Risk: Low – only includes optional data, which is not mandatory but adds value when available.

Rationale: Including optional but valuable sustainability data enhances transparency and may give the organization a reputational advantage in an increasingly eco-conscious market.
Organization benefits: Enhances market differentiation and supports value-adding goals.
Source: O3, O2
Priority: Should – aligns with user's aim of ecosystem-level compliance and supports a proactive sustainability approach.

System Verification Success Criteria: At least one product includes an additional sustainability marker in its DPP.

ID 11 Statement: The organization should establish a simple internal procedure for verifying the accuracy of DPP data before sharing it with downstream operators. Risk: Low – can be done manually using a checklist or basic review by a second person.

Rationale: A verification process reduces the risk of errors and ensures the reliability of the DPP data provided.
Organization benefits: Increases confidence in the DPP data and reduces the risk of rework or disputes.
Source: O1, O4
Priority: Should – supports data quality and is aligned with the user’s role in the supply chain.

System Verification Success Criteria: A documented verification step exists in the DPP process, and it is applied consistently.

ID 12 Statement: The organization should provide downstream partners with a brief summary or overview of the DPP data structure and content. Risk: Low – can be done using a simple text document or email summary.

Rationale: Clear communication about the format and content of the DPP helps downstream partners understand and use the data effectively.
Organization benefits: Reduces confusion and support requests, leading to smoother collaboration.
Source: O5
Priority: Should – supports user’s aim of ecosystem-level compliance.

System Verification Success Criteria: At least one downstream partner receives a summary document outlining the DPP data.

ID 13 Statement: The organization could explore the use of low-cost QR codes or digital tags to link physical products to their DPP data. Risk: Medium – may require investment in scanning technology and design, but can be low-cost solutions.

Rationale: Digital tags provide an easy and cost-effective way to enable direct access to product data, supporting transparency and consumer engagement.
Organization benefits: Improves traceability and potentially increases consumer trust and engagement.
Source: O2
Priority: Could – not mandatory but offers value in improving accessibility and customer trust.

System Verification Success Criteria: A sample product is tagged with a QR code that successfully directs to its DPP data.

ID 14 Statement: The organization could participate in industry or sector-wide DPP platforms or consortiums to reduce the cost and complexity of data sharing. Risk: Medium – depends on the availability and suitability of shared platforms for small suppliers.

Rationale: Shared platforms can standardize data formats, reduce duplication of effort, and improve interoperability across the supply chain.
Organization benefits: Reduces implementation burden and supports collaboration with peers.
Source: O3, O2
Priority: Could – supports digital maturity growth and aligns with ecosystem-level compliance.

System Verification Success Criteria: The organization is registered with or has joined one shared DPP platform.

ID 15 Statement: The organization could provide training to employees on the importance and handling of DPP data. Risk: Low – training can be informal and based on existing documentation.

Rationale: Employee awareness ensures consistent application of DPP procedures, reduces errors, and supports long-term compliance.
Organization benefits: Enhances internal capacity and reduces risk of human error.
Source: O5
Priority: Could – supports continuous improvement and helps prepare for future digital maturity.

System Verification Success Criteria: At least one employee completes a basic training session on DPP procedures.

ID 16 Statement: The organization should not attempt to collect data that is beyond its control or for which it has no access (e.g., downstream use or disposal information). Risk: Low – this limitation is realistic and aligns with the user’s size and digital maturity.

Rationale: Attempting to collect such data would be burdensome, inaccurate, and not required by current regulations.
Organization benefits: Focuses efforts on data within control and avoids unnecessary complexity.
Source: O1, O2
Priority: Should not – avoids overcommitment and aligns with the supplier’s role in the supply chain.

System Verification Success Criteria: No attempt is made to collect or report on lifecycle stages beyond the supplier’s direct control.
